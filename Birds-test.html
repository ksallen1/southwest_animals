Birds

The birds of the Southwest come in all shapes, sizes, and lifestyles.


The Great Horned Owl is of low concern status, but has seen population decline in the US.

Turkey Vultures are not currently a threatened species. They do face risks of poisoning when consuming carcasses containing Lead and other contaminants.

The Gambel's Quail population is stable, but facing some habitat degradation.

Birds of the Southwest

  • Great Horned Owl (Bubo virginianus)
  • Turkey vultures (Cathartes aura)
  • Gambel's Quail (Callipepla gambelii)

Bird facts!

Owls are nocturnal predators, and can see clearly in the dark with a few percent of the light that humans need.

The Gambel's Quail keeps a single mate for its entire lifetime!

A Turkey Vulture's stomach acid is strong, and has a pH around 1, so it can digest just about anything safely!

Features

Bird physical descriptions
The Great Horned Owl receives its name from a pair of feather tufts on top of its head. This owl is also identifed audibly by its iconic growls and screeches.
Great Horned Owls stand about 18 inches tall and weigh around three pounds.

A Gabel's Quail stands about 10 inches tall, and weights less than half a pound. These birds have an iconic ploom of feathers sticking out of their heads.

Diet

Typical Diet of Desert Birds

The diets of desert birds differs between predatory, scavenger, and non-predatory species

    Predatory Birds:

  1. Mice
  2. Smal birds
  3. Reptiles
  4. Fish
  5. Rabbits
  6. Carrion of the above For scavengers)

    Non-predatory Birds:

  1. Seeds
  2. Fruit
  3. Leaves and Greens
  4. Insects

Bird feeding fact!

Birds are immune to the capsaicin reaction, so they can comfortably eat and spread the seeds of spicy peppers, like the jalapeno!

Predators

Bird predators and competition.

Predatory birds, like raptors will often prey on their smaller counterparts, especially groud-dwelling birds.
Birds often compete with each other for food, especially species that prey on insects.
