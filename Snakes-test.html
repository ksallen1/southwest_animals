Snakes

The American Southwest is home to many different species of snakes.

Ecologically, snakes play an important role--acting as a predator for pest populations, and serving as prey for larger creatures.

Snakes of the Southwest

  • Western Diamond-backed Rattlesnake (Crotalus Atrox)
  • Arizona Coral Snake(Micruroides euryxanthus)
  • Mexican Hog-nosed Snake(Heterodon nasicus kennerlyi)

Snake facts!

Twenty types of venomous snake live just within Arizona!

Caution: An untreated rattlesnake bite is fatal 10-20% of the time!

The Diamondback Rattlesnake is the most common rattler encountered by people and found in developed areas.

Features

Snake physical descriptions
All of Arizona's venemous snakes have anguluar heads.
Rattlesnakes come with a specialized set of loose scales on the end of their tails--a rattle. This rattle makes an iconic, startling noise used to scare away would-be predators.

Diet

Typical Diet of Desert Snakes

  1. Mice
  2. Rabbits
  3. Birds
  4. Fish
  5. Lizards (eaten typically by younger snakes)
  6. Insects (rarer)
  7. Eggs (rarer)

In addition to being active at night (nocturnal), many snakes are active around dusk and dawn (crepuscular).

Snake feeding fact:
Desert snakes can survive multiple months or even a year or two without eating!

Predators

Snakes, both venomous and non-venomous have a variety of predators. Larger grazing or herd animals, like cows, deer, and horses will trample the snakes, which are perceived as a threat.
Other creatures, like bobcats, hawks, and roadrunners will eat snakes as prey.
